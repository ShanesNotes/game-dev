The Wolf: Simple Chase AI

An enemy that hunts you. "AI" sounds scary — it's really just a tiny bit of vector maths, run every frame.

What "chase" actually means

Strip away the mystique: to chase someone you need two facts — where am I and where are they — then you step toward them. Every frame the wolf will:

  1. Find the direction from itself to the player.
  2. Move that way at its speed.
  3. Face the way it's heading.

The one new tool: direction between two points

global_position

A node's position in the world (not relative to a parent). Use it when comparing the locations of two different nodes.

vector subtraction

player.global_position - global_position gives a Vector2 that points from the wolf to the player. Subtracting positions is how you get "the way from A to B".

normalized()

That vector's length depends on how far apart they are. .normalized() shrinks it to length 1 — pure direction, no distance — so multiplying by speed gives an even pace whatever the gap. (This is the same fix for the Lesson 3 diagonal-speed bug!)

Part A — build the wolf

I made you assets/wolf.png. Build this as a child of Main (it's a moving creature, so it's a CharacterBody2D like the player):

▸ Wolf (CharacterBody2D) ├─ Sprite2D (wolf.png) └─ CollisionShape2D (its body)
1

Right-click Main → Add Child Node → CharacterBody2D → rename Wolf.

2

Add child Sprite2D → Texture → Quick Load → assets/wolf.png. Leave its name as Sprite2D (code relies on it).

3

Add child CollisionShape2D → New RectangleShape2D → size over the wolf.

4

Drag the Wolf in the viewport to a far corner of the grass, away from the player.

Part B — the chase script

1

Select Wolf → Attach Script → Create (e.g. wolf.gd), and use:

GDScript · wolf.gd
extends CharacterBody2D

var speed = 120
@onready var player = get_node("../Player")
@onready var sprite = $Sprite2D

func _physics_process(delta):
    var direction = (player.global_position - global_position).normalized()
    velocity = direction * speed
    move_and_slide()
    sprite.flip_h = velocity.x < 0
get_node("../Player") — reach a node by its path. .. means "go up to my parent" (Main), then down into Player. Now player refers to your character. (Same idea as $, but reaching a sibling.)
(player.global_position - global_position).normalized() — the whole brain of the chase. Subtract to get the arrow from wolf→player; normalize to keep it pure direction.
velocity = direction * speed + move_and_slide() — same movement you already know — the wolf just aims at the player instead of reading keys.
sprite.flip_h = velocity.x < 0 — mirror the sprite when moving left. The wolf art faces right; flip_h flips it horizontally. velocity.x < 0 is true when heading left, so flip_h becomes true exactly then.
◈ why speed 120?

Your player moves at 200. A slower wolf (120) means it pursues but you can outrun it — that gap is a design choice that makes the chase tense instead of hopeless. Tune it and feel the difference.

▶

Press F5. The wolf turns toward you and gives chase wherever you run. 🎉 The hunt is on.
