Why tiles run your favourite games

Zelda, Stardew, Pokémon — their entire worlds are built from a small set of little square images ("tiles") stamped over a grid, thousands of times. Reusing a handful of 32×32 pictures to build a whole map is wildly efficient, and Godot has a dedicated system for it.

Three words for today

Tile

One small square image — e.g. a patch of grass. Your building block.

TileSet

The palette: a collection of tiles, sliced from one image (an "atlas"). You'll load assets/tileset.png — a 128×32 sheet with four 32-pixel tiles: grass, dirt, stone, and a grass variant.

TileMapLayer

The canvas: a node you paint tiles onto with the mouse. It stores your whole map cheaply, no matter how big.

◈ I made you a tileset

Look in the FileSystem dock (bottom-left) for assets/tileset.png — a 128×32 strip of four 32-pixel tiles (grass | dirt | stone | grass variant) I generated into your project. We'll slice it into tiles in a moment.

Part A — add the canvas

1

Right-click Main → Add Child Node → search TileMapLayer → Create. Rename it Ground.

2

Put it behind everything. In the Scene dock, drag Ground so it's the first child of Main (above Player and Wall). Siblings lower in the list draw on top — so the floor must sit at the top of the list to render underneath the robot.

Part B — build the TileSet (slice the image)

1

Select Ground. In the Inspector, find Tile Set → click <empty> → New TileSet.

2

Click the new TileSet to expand it and set Tile Size to 32 × 32 (the default is 16 — our tiles are 32).

3

A TileSet panel opens at the bottom of the screen. In its lower-left "Sources" box, click + → Atlas (or just drag assets/tileset.png from FileSystem into that box).

4

Godot asks "Automatically create tiles in the atlas?" → Yes. It slices the image into your four tiles. (If it doesn't ask, click the atlas, then the three-dots/⋮ menu → Create Tiles Automatically.)

Part C — paint!

1

At the bottom, switch from the TileSet tab to the TileMap tab. You'll see your four tiles in the palette.

2

Click the grass tile to select it, then left-click and drag across the viewport to paint a big grass field under (and around) the robot.

3

Select the dirt tile and paint a path winding through the grass. Try stone for a rocky patch. Right-click erases a tile.

4

Press F5 and walk the robot around on your floor. 🎉 It's starting to look like a game.

◇ notice

You can paint a thousand tiles and the map is still tiny in memory — it just stores "tile #0 at grid cell (3,4)". That efficiency is exactly why tile-based worlds dominate 2D games.
